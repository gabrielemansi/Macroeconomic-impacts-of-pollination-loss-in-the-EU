Principal contributions, originality, and conceptual advance of the paper.

Methodological advance: 
This is the first integrated assessment to propagate a multi-model, climate-driven pollinator species distribution ensemble 
through a spatially explicit computable general equilibrium model at sub-national resolution, across multiple emission and socioeconomic scenarios to 2070. 
Existing pollinator-economics studies either impose stylised binary collapse shocks at national resolution, or combine static pollination-sufficiency proxies with land-use scenarios; 
neither captures the dynamic, climate-driven evolution of pollinator ranges propagated to economy-wide outcomes at the scale at which decisions are made.

Unprecedented data integration:
The framework couples 18 SSP–RCP scenarios covering 153 wild pollinator species with high-resolution data for 129 crops and a CGE model resolved across 122 NUTS regions.
No prior assessment in this domain has achieved comparable spatial, sectoral, and scenario coverage over this temporal horizon.

Robust treatment of uncertainty:
Findings are systematically tested across 18 scenarios spanning multiple species distribution models, emission pathways, and socioeconomic assumptions, with full sensitivity analysis.
The directional robustness of the central result holds across the entire ensemble despite substantial inter-model spread.

Conceptual significance beyond pollination: 
The paper demonstrates that spatial aggregation systematically conceals the damages from biodiversity-driven productivity shocks, with regional impacts two to five times greater than national averages. 
This is a general property of how standard integrated assessment frameworks interact with the joint distribution of ecosystem service supply and economic dependence. 
The implication is that the existing macroeconomic literature has been systematically underestimating the spatial concentration of climate–biodiversity damages, 
a result with consequences for all ecosystem services characterised by geographic heterogeneity, not pollination alone.

Practical utility and policy relevance:
Outputs are produced at the scale at which agricultural and conservation policy is implemented, and are openly available in formats accessible to non-academic users:
regional administrations, agricultural ministries, sectoral and producer associations, financial institutions, and farmer cooperatives.
This makes the work directly relevant to current implementation of the Nature Restoration Regulation and to ongoing IPBES and IPCC processes on climate–biodiversity interactions. 
A substantial portion of the published research in this field remains effectively inaccessible to these users, either because spatial resolution is too coarse, because data are not openly shared, 
or because outputs require specialist translation. Our framework was designed from the outset to address that structural gap.
